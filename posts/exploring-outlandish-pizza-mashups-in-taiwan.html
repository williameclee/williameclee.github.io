Cuisine Fusion and Cultural Politics

Exploring Outlandish Pizza Mashups in Taiwan

En-Chi (William) Lee

April 12th, 2024

Italians, with a deep-rooted passion, consider their pizzas not just a dish but the art of making them a cultural heritage [1]. Consequently, few dishes stir up more controversy than Hawaiian pizza: some criticise it as cultural appreciation, and Italy’s ambassadors in the UK were offended by such an abomination [2, 3]. On the other hand, many people love the taste of it, and the president of Iceland once had to formally apologise for wishing he could ban it [4].

Pizza Hut Taiwan [5]
Pizza Hut pushed out the ‘QQ’ black sugar boba milk tea pizza in Taiwan in 2020, sparking online discussions and attracting international attention.

Yet, pineapple and ham on pizzas are far from the most ‘sacrilegious’ toppings in the world. In Taiwan, the American fast food chain Pizza Hut has been offering limited-time pizzas with toppings inspired by local and international cuisines since 2019, including boba milk tea [5] in the same year and Japanese tonkatsu ramen in 2020 [6,7]. Since then, it has featured at least twelve Taiwanese street food icons in its pizzas. While this seems like a celebration of Taiwan’s culinary diversity and creativity, a closer look at the menu reveals interesting patterns. Specifically, what ethnic cuisines are represented—and what are not—in the menu and how they are marketed to the public demonstrate the cultural identity Taiwan wants to present to the world and the internal struggles it faces.

Fast Food as an Agent of Globalisation and Fusion

Pizza Hut Taiwan [5]
Pizza Hut Taiwan’s latest specialty, the ‘Cheese and double tsingtuan turtle’ pizza, which hardly looks like a pizza anymore.

A dish can have distinct variations and cultural meanings in different cultures [8], and pizza is no exception. In Naples, it is a simple and traditional dish, while in the US, it has turned into a fast food staple. On the other hand, pizza in East Asia is commonly known for its extravagant toppings and is often ordered for special occasions. This is a historical legacy of how fast-food chains popularised the American diet in the region and how local food culture has modified it to fit the local palate. In the past, Chinese people used to queue for hours to dine in KFC! As a dish adapts to different cultures, it can become highly far removed from its original form. For example, Pizza Hut Taiwan’s latest ‘Cheese and double tsingtuan turtle’ pizza [9] does not even resemble the round shape of a pizza anymore.

With its international variability, pizza has become a way to exchange and hybridise different cultures and cuisines. The story behind Pizza Hut Taiwan’s ramen pizza is an excellent example of the culinary interaction between Japan and Taiwan [7]. In 2019, the Taiwan-originated boba milk tea took over Japan overnight, and the Japanese incorporated it into ramen, rice, and even spicy tofu dishes. The absurd phenomenon was widely discussed in both nations. Pizza Hut Taiwan took advantage of this trend. It created the ‘Love by first bite’ ramen pizza, collaborating with the famous Japanese ramen restaurant Menya Musashi, which was also behind the bubble milk tea tsukemen [10]. As Pizza Hut continued to push out unconventional pizzas in Taiwan, their Japanese branch was inspired to create a coriander pizza in 2023, which also became a hit [11]. Pizza has become a medium through which the two countries communicate and exchange culinary ideas.

However, it is equally important to recognise that cultural and food hybridity is not always harmonious and can carry political implications. When one culture holds more power, the minority may be forced to transform or become marginalised [12]. This was the sad situation of Taiwanese cuisine in the 1950s to ‘80s, after the Kuomintang (KMT) government retreated from China to Taiwan. At the time, Taiwanese people had developed a unique culture after 50 years of Japanese colonisation. The cultural difference created tension between the KMT and the local people, eventually leading to the Martial Law period [13].

During this time, KMT superimposed a new wave of Mainland Chinese culture, including the language and customs, on the Taiwanese people. Food was also affected. Chinese cuisines were promoted and dominated state banquets, while Taiwanese cuisines were degraded to a mere collection of dishes [14]. The status lasted until the 1990s when martial law was discarded, Taiwan democratised, and the local culture revitalised. After that, local cuisines reoccupied their place in Taiwanese food culture and rapidly globalised and hybridised with other food cultures. This has resulted in a diverse culinary landscape where the boba pizza was born.

What do the Pizza Hut toppings tell us about Taiwan’s culture?

Pizza Hut in Taiwan consistently introduces limited-time pizzas featuring outlandish toppings. Most of them are inspired by Taiwanese street food, such as stinky tofu and oyster omelette, and people are crazy about them. Antony Leung, the General Manager of Pizza Hut Taiwan, shared with Taiwan Business Topics that their marketing team discovered that ‘local ingredients are the best driver for social buzz’ [15]. While they have started exploring cuisines from other countries, their ‘Best of Taiwan street food series’ continues to be the crowd favourite. Their pizza fusion has earned international attention, being featured on CNN [6], and inspiring their competitor, Domino’s, to create their version of boba milk tea pizza [16].

Even if it’s purely a marketing gimmick, the new pizza has successfully stirred de- bate on the Internet.

CNN[6]

However, what is considered ‘Taiwanese’ is sometimes a complex political issue. While Pizza Hut often playfully teases the Italians in its commercials, it is careful not to stir controversies. The local dishes and ingredients it incorporates into its pizzas are familiar to all Taiwanese. In fact, they are found in virtually every night market and are enjoyed by most people (except coriander!).

Therefore, the toppings on these pizzas reflect the mainstream perception of Taiwanese food culture. However, they don’t fully represent the diversity of local cuisines. Thirteen of the sixteen examined topping flavours draw inspiration from dishes rooted intimately in Taiwan; the remaining three originated from China but have been assimilated into the Taiwanese palate long ago. That is precisely the issue—the ethnic and modern Chinese cuisines that entered Taiwan after WWII are missing.

Due to the political tension, it becomes understandable why Pizza Hut does not use contemporary Chinese food items as toppings despite their potential popularity. Instead, the Chinese-origin dishes it features, like tang-yuan and tsung-tzu, have adapted and been localised over time. They are now considered integral to the Taiwanese, or at least the Holko culture.

Taiwan is home to four main ethnic groups [21,22]: the indigenous tribes, the Holko and Hakka people who migrated from southeast China in the 17th–19th centuries, and the Chinese Mainlanders who retreated to Taiwan with the KMT in the 1940s and ‘50s [23,24,25]. The Holko is now the culturally dominant group in Taiwan. They are the ‘non-ethnic’ [12] group in Taiwan, with their language and food culture almost synonymous with ‘Taiwanese’ [21]. The indigenous and Hakka cuisines have unique characteristics that separate them from the Holko. Yet, they have never been featured in Pizza Hut’s ‘Best of Taiwan street food series.’

Differentiating Taiwanese from Chinese influences

Taiwan and China have a complex relationship; many of their citizens share languages, cultures, and ancestry. Nonetheless, after years of separation, the two sides have developed divergent national identities. They are now ruled by two independent governments, leading to political tension. According to a 2024 survey by the Election Study Centre [26] at National Chengchi University in Taiwan, over 60% of Taiwanese people identify exclusively as Taiwanese, and only 3% as solely Chinese. This desire for independence conflicts with the Chinese government’s insistence that Taiwan is a part of China. Even cuisines are caught up in this political struggle, being used as a channel for governments to promote their agenda. For instance, Chunying Hua, the spokesperson of China’s Ministry of Foreign Affairs, tweeted in 2022 that Taiwan has always been a part of China because an online map service showed 100 traditional Chinese restaurants in Taipei, Taiwan’s capital [27].

Many Taiwanese people and the government are not flattered by this statement. Since the political indigenisation and democratisation, local ingredients have appeared much more frequently in state banquets. Taiwanese specialities are also incorporated to deepen the connection between the government and the land and to differentiate Taiwan from China [14,28]. The owner of the HoSu restaurant in Taipei told The New York Times journalist Li Yuan that he believes his Taiwanese dishes can help people to stand up against China:

We have to help people identify with this land so a national identity could emerge eventually. So if something happens, we will be willing to fight for our homeland.

Ian Lee, owner and executive chef of the HoSu Restaurant [29]

Modern Chinese food, especially snacks that have gained popularity on social media in China, while sometimes also becoming popular among some demographics, have faced backlash when introduced to Taiwan. Around the Lunar New Year in 2023, the convenience food luo-shi-fen sparked anger among Taiwanese when customers discovered pro-Chinese government slogans on the packaging [30]. Similarly, mo-yu-shuang, a popular Chinese snack among elementary school students in Taiwan in the same year, raised parents’ and teachers’ concerns about its high sugar content and potential health risks [31]. People are more sceptical of Chinese food. To them, this resistance is about politics but also preserving cultural values.

Missing ethnic cuisines on the menu: The Hakka and indigenous

Taiwanese cuisine reflects the island’s cultural history and complex identity formation, and the Hakka and indigenous people have profound connections to their land. However, despite often being at the forefront of political discussions concerning what it means to be Taiwanese, these cuisines are absent from Pizza Hut’s ‘Best of Taiwan street food series.’

Compared to the Chinese or ‘non-ethnic’ Taiwanese cuisines, the Hakka and indigenous people have historically had to fight harder to promote and distinguish themselves [21,22]. When arguing for the independence of Taiwanese culture, indigenous tribes are often put forth to demonstrate the uniqueness of Taiwan [28]. Food is a convenient symbol in the discussions to convey identity and culture [22]. As Kwang Ok Kim, a professor of anthropology at the Seoul National University, opens his book on modern East Asian cuisines with,

Food, however, is laden with much deeper meanings than simply providing neces- sary nutritional and medicinal results. It is a space for social and cultural practice. . . . Numerous studies have approached food as a mechanism to materialise modes of thought and to express a group’s identity, cultural system, or social classificatory system.

Kwang Ok Kim, in Re-Orienting Cuisine East Asian Foodways in the Twenty-First Century [33]

The symbolic significance of food is not unnoticed by politicians. Following political movements, the Hakka and indigenous communities and cuisines have been increasingly recognised in state banquets since the 2000s [14,22].

However, this recognition comes with its challenges. The food historian Yu-Jen Chen at National Taiwan Normal University has voiced concerns that pursuing a culinary identity might impose boundaries around these cuisines and limit their evolution [22]. Additionally, in the global trend of modernisation, some characteristics that define these cuisines, like the saltiness of Hakka dishes, may be considered undesirable or signify a lower social status in the mainstream culture [22]. Moreover, there is a possibility of being romanticised, as seen with the portrayal of indigenous cuisine as being ‘simple’ and ‘natural’ [28]. Striking a balance between honouring the artificially defined ‘authentic’ practices and adapting to modern tastes and lifestyles is an ongoing struggle for these cuisines [22].

The cuisines that are specific to individual ethnic groups might not be easily incorporated into a unified Taiwanese narrative that relates to the daily life of the Holko people, who make up most of the population. Therefore, there is scepticism towards the authenticity of politicians’ recognition of these cuisines. A Hakka greengrocer told Chen that he does not think ‘those politicians really care about Hakka affairs. Yes they care, but only during the election campaigns’ [21]. It becomes a paradox that these ‘ethnic’ cuisines are simultaneously overrepresented to demonstrate the diversity and independence of Taiwanese culture and marginalised in the pursuit of a coherent and relatable Taiwanese identity that can be used during election or marketing campaigns.

The paradox might explain why Hakka and indigenous cuisines are missing from Pizza Hut Taiwan’s limited-time offerings. Their dishes might be considered too closely associated with their respective groups to be hybridised with pizza; alternatively, they might not be deemed safe choices for a fast food chain aiming to maximise its customer base.

Pizza Hu Taiwan [36]
Pizza Hut Taiwan introduced the ‘Zero damage’ pizza on 2023’s April Fool’s Day, which is a pizza with no toppings ‘to not offend anyone’.

Taiwan has come a long way from the Japanese colonial era to the end of the martial law period. Now, it is building its cultural and culinary identity through cuisine and happily displaying it to the world by embracing fusion. While giant food chains are homogenising the world’s diet [8], Pizza Hut Taiwan’s outlandish pizza toppings show that there is a way to showcase a culture’s uniqueness with these fusion abominations. In an era of social media, these eye-catching products have also become a channel for international culture exchange, leaving perhaps Italians as the only group fuming.

It is a shame that Pizza Hut Taiwan has yet to incorporate the flavours of Hakka and indigenous dishes, which are true culinary treasures. The absence of these options, along with contemporary Chinese dishes, as pizza toppings not only reflects a desire to differentiate Taiwanese cuisine from Chinese influences but also highlights the marginalisation of certain culinary traditions. As Taiwanese, we have a responsibility to integrate these culinary gems into our daily lives. When that day comes, it will not only signify a step closer to a more diverse and inclusive culinary scene but also a more equal ethnic politics scene.
